### 4. Problem Exploration Narrative
Cotne’s interview provided the most significant "reality check" for our project. As a student with a technical background, he actually built a **Telegram bot** to solve the coordination issue. It failed within three weeks—not because of the code, but because of **behavioral friction**. 

The organizer refused to leave Messenger to post updates. This highlighted a massive constraint: the "Supply Side" (organizers) will not change their workflow for a secondary tool. Cotne also tried a Google Sheets solution, which failed because it went "stale" the moment the organizer stopped updating it. This narrative confirms that our product cannot be an "extra step"; it must integrate into how organizers already work.

### 5. Mom Test Compliance Check
* **Pass:** I spent a significant amount of time asking about his *failed* solutions. By asking "Why did the Telegram bot stop being used?" I moved past his pride in building it and got to the hard truth about organizer adoption.
* **Fail:** Because Cotne is a friend, I occasionally fell into "brainstorming mode," where we talked about how a new app *could* work. This is a Mom Test error (collecting fantasies instead of facts).
* **Correction:** In the future, I will cut off "What if" conversations by saying: "That sounds like a cool idea, but let's go back to why the Google Sheet didn't work last month."

### 6. Personal Reflection
Cotne’s interview completely changed my perspective on our MVP. I realized that **the organizer is the gatekeeper**. If the organizer finds the tool even slightly annoying, the players never get the information. It shifted my focus from "cool features for players" to "extreme simplicity for organizers."
